Oni

  • First Aid - a couple of more people needed if we expand OR first aid crew not moved to other tasks. Ideally, always two and in pairs. Possibility of St Johns Covering for free next time. Best pursue this.
  • Second Jasper's point on clearly defined roles. Overstepping boundaries with suggestions meant extra stress and less efficiency.
  • Shift leader, deputy shift leader and clear shifts. Jonty and Russ need to let go a little somewhat and delegate to their opposite number and teams below. I felt there was a certain amount of micromanaging on some tasks.
  • Second Will's point. Network team picked up too much of the 'site planning' - need a site manager
  • Proper briefings for volunteers.
  • Another van and at least 2 more drivers - moving stuff needs a rota
  • Minibus drivers - better rota and more drivers.
  • Meeting in London is no bad thing; a physical meeting somewhere regular is fine but delegating down to local teams is definitely needed. The meetings in London had too much minutiae and were too often.
  • Insist that people who talk should pay and register a week before and if they dont, they are pulled from the talk schedule. There seemed to be a lot of rescheduling.
  • Taking stock meeting every 8 hours or so, at shift change.
  • We had a lot of chancers try and sign up for first aid and we culled them down by asking them to provide certs before they were accepted. A similar method for commitment needs to be applied to speakers. See the tickets idea above.
  • T-Shirts and bags *should not be screen printed* - we can get a better result for less time and effort and only a little more money. Screen printing is a fun workshop activity but compare the professional looking tilda to the t-shirts.
  • DONT do massive shiny projects and attempt to finish them on site - I think hipster and I came close but it made me stressy and I spent less time on actual setup.

Naxxfish

  • +1 on physical meetings - those not at the meetings were sometimes left out from other work being done, tasks being delegated. Proper agenda and minutes would have certainly helped this - so that even if you're not there, you know what was asked to be done, and if any of the attendees have picked it up. Also, circulate the agenda with plenty of time before the meeting to allow non-attending people to share comments via email before the meeting.
  • Strategic meetings can be less frequent (say once or twice a month) and individual teams can call meetings as their practical work progresses - rather than picking over the details weekly.
  • +1 on clearly defined roles. Not just Infra team picking up the slack, I think everyone else did to some degree as well.
  • Having a list of core teams that are required at the start of planning is the way forward. We were, I think, missing some - e.g. AV - which seemed to be mostly Charles and I in the end.
  • Find out realistically what roles are required for volunteers from *all* teams before setting the it into stone. For AV/Streaming we weren't included in the volunteer system, which ended up being crazy hard to manage! I ended up running most of the AV myself until we found ad-hoc volunteers - not good! Delayed a talk on Saturday morning because I was a single point of failure.
    • SamLR there was an entire category "Stage" who were supposed to be helping. I didn't roll the streaming stuff in because it was constantly sold as something EMFM were going to handle. I think this collapses down to 'better volunteer management' and yes it will be better
  • On the subject of volunteers, we should not schedule talks when we don't know if there are the staff to do it. At minimum, one person should be posted at each venue whenever talks are on.
  • A lot of volunteers made themselves known half way through, and we didn't know what to do with them. We need some mechanism of dispatching extra help to people who need it when it becomes available, and making use of all the volunteer capacity we have.
  • If we're going to do something like streaming and recording talks - we need to do it properly or not at all. We ended up with quite a bit of kit, but a lot of it didn't work, or didn't work well, and was inconsistent at the best of times. As great as improvised solutions are, you can't rely on them to work consistently for long periods of time. We need to be able to have a tested solution ready to roll out from the get go.
  • Again, if we're going to do streaming, we need to know that we have permission from the speakers. This was only a problem twice, however it did trip me up the first time a little, and ended up taking EMFM off air temporarily after making a dodgey firewall rule.
  • Information! People need it. Here's a list of things that I would have liked to know:
    • How to get there
      • Public/private transport.
      • Including reputable local taxi companies - we could even get a discount if we recommend them
      • When do the shuttle busses run? Need a timetable - or at least a way of finding out when the next one will be
      • What is the train timetable to/from popular destinations
    • Where
      • Where should I camp?
      • Where are the venues?
      • Where are the workshops?
      • Where are the other villages, so I can look for interesting stuff
      • Where are events going on in the evening?
      • Surrounding area:
        • Supermarkets
        • Hardware shops
        • Resturants/bars/stuff in the local area if you want to explore a bit.
    • Who to contact
      • If something Bad(tm) Happened, who do you get hold of first.
        • First aid is fairly obvious, but what if someone was a victim of crime, spotted something dangerous, lost their child, etc. etc.
        • Volunteer shirts are good, but did people know that they should ask us about it?
      • If I want to volunteer, who do I talk to?
  • Site planning is important. Whilst the villages do grow organically, the larger and more demanding ones should be known about in advance (e.g. hackspace villages). The later notice there is, the less we are likely to be able to provide them in terms of infrastructure, definitive locations and area, etc.
  • People like to be told roughly where to camp. You can also use this to balance out the load on infra, and avoid the problems we had with capacity.
  • Whilst flexibility and organic growth is good - that growth needs to be inside a simple overall structure to make it work well. A bit like a trellis with ivy growing up it. Without it, the ivy grows in a mess on the floor, but with it it can climb up and get bigger and better. The trellis doesn't need the ivy to be P-clipped to it to force it to grow in the right direction, but it does need something to hang off without over crowding itself.</analogy> So what I'm saying is - we should not try to over-plan and schedule the event - but we should provide a framework such that it doesn't become a confusing mess when we let people do their own thing.

Russ

  • Planning:
    • Meetings were too frequent, too full of irrelevant details. We got better at them, but I still think they weren't great.
    • A lot of this was chasing people up. Some people were not proactive at organising things, and chasing that up was frustrating.
    • We should make it clear that members of the core organising team have to pull their weight, because next time we'll have far less margin for that.
    • We have much better knowledge of what needs organising when for next time.
  • Details of suppliers need to be recorded somewhere accessible to everyone. There were way too many SPOFs in peoples' heads.
  • Ticket sales:
    • If we're supplying chairs/tables/hay/tents we should have a way of charging for these through the standard ticket sales system. Individually collecting everything is a massive pain.
  • Gate:
    • We were right at the limit of our ability to deal with tickets based on lists of names on paper - next time we need an electronic system. Needs to be able to answer the question of how many people are on site at any given time.
    • There was talk of people in reflective jackets walking in without wristbands being checked. Gate staff need more training + a short manual.
    • Separate pedestrian and vehicle gates might be a good idea.
    • Crowd barriers at the gate to funnel people through. Separate staff for re-entry and wristband pickup at peak times.
  • Site Transport
    • Golf cart and telehandler were both invaluable
    • Instead of the golf cart, next time we should get something slightly quieter (you can hear the golf cart in lots of the talk videos!) and with a more robust trailer - John Deere Gator or similar.
    • Site speed limit should apply to all vehicles.
    • All vehicles moving at night should have lights on!
    • Someone got injured on the golf cart. No pissing about on vehicles.
  • First aid: we *definitely* need cover on the closing night - that was a big omission. We should also have cover for teardown.
  • Transport:
    • Get as much stuff delivered as possible. I would definitely aim to try and get beer/bar stuff delivered on pallets. The best way of reducing the amount of annoyance caused by the van is to reduce the number of runs it needs to do.
    • Next time, find somewhere which doesn't need us to run a park and ride!
  • Bar:
    • Next time we need to sell spirits and cider.
    • Budgeted for an ale:lager ratio of 3:1, then only bought half the amount of lager and didn't sell all of that. Geeks like ale. But likely more people would have bought lager if it was draught, so next time we should probably sell keg beer.
    • Ice and water.
  • Infrastructure:
    • We lost a cable, but what really sucked is that we didn't notice it. We need to improve process to make sure we spot these things earlier.
    • Need to lock down the power plan (and other things) at least a month in advance so we can get as much as possible from the same supplier.
  • Teardown:
    • It *was* long enough. The reason we overran on Tuesday was because there weren't enough vans (or alternatively there was too much crap).
    • Needs planning before the event.
    • Would be nice to have a time for when infrastructure was switched off. I needed a shower and didn't get one.
    • Potentially consider deferring taking down all the core infrastructure (including showers) until the last day.
  • Money:
    • Arrange the cash float way in advance.
    • Get change bags.
  • Things to make sure we have loads of:
    • Cable ties.
      • Use http://www.rapstrap.com there reusable and removes need for cutters. NottingHack might be able to get them sponsored --'RepRap' Matt (talk) 20:59, 4 September 2012 (UTC)
    • Side cutters for cutting cable ties.
    • Scaff spanners.
  • UAV safety:
    • Separate area for flying vehicles (with possibility to fly outside that area with permission?)
    • These should probably have lights on at night too.
  • Ticket refunds:
    • After a couple of people moaning I decided to promise to refund tickets after the event if we made a surplus.
    • This kind of sucks, though, especially if people hear about it. What's the best refund policy? Perhaps only refund up until n months before the event?
  • More stuff to come...

Martind

Staff

We need someone to do logistics full-time. Too many people frantically running around trying to establish what's going on, and who knows more.

  • are shuttle buses going? who is allowed to drive?
  • similar questions about shop runs.
  • etc.

And: a team for site management. Signage, tents, etc.

Some staff messaging system or daybook that actually captures people's needs and announcements ("making a trip to London tonight") so we don't have to rely on natural chatter for things to bubble through. A short-term backlog (capturing the next few hours.) This is why: https://twitter.com/tef/status/242364342052474881 (why's this a tweet and not a note in a daybook?) Or alternatively a clearly defined shift leader that the team reports to.

Need to thoroughly brief staff on how to manage their own energy. Focus, attention span, distractions, making time for rest, getting sleep, getting food. Spending much time training others to spread the load. Not spending time interrupting others unless absolutely needed.

Can't keep sticking stuff on tent walls. We need *many* *big* *proper* whiteboards and panels. And pens. And Gaffa tape. Stuff like that cannot ever be the bottleneck.

Community

We need volunteers on stand-by, e.g. stationed near the HQ tent. Or at the gate. Lots of random tasks coming up. (This could be a strong driver for collaborative projects: work together with great people to set up amazing things. E.g. the family & kids area. Stuff like this could be announced at the opening ceremony so we get lots of help, and good implementation ideas.)

We need camp whiteboards so people can leave messages, make announcements etc. E.g. for shop runs, workshops, ...

Generally should make use of the camp setting for more communal projects. Atm we're very focused on keeping everything running in the first place; would love to now go beyond that and start some larger collaboration experiments. (Much of that is happening in particular niches, for example the badge team received incredible community support this weekend in addressing some last-minute issues.)

BBC video from day 1 is very nice -- but: no women! It's an all-men video! Need to change that. Next time: speak to Sue Black etc early to get many many women groups to participate.

Org Structure

It appears NottingHack is offering to run all the workshop/hacking areas for next time. This is an excellent idea. Can we identify more similarly self-contained camp areas and outsource them as a whole to existing (well-trusted) communities? With some well-defined boundaries and well-expressed expectations this could radically simplify our planning process, and spread the load quite a lot.

Jake/gmjhowe

I was essentially involved with some of the marketing leading up to the event, specifically from a design/print management point of view. I attended the event, but aside from delivering the booklets, I had no involvement with running the event, so items relating to the camp in general are those of an attendee only.

Marketing, Design and Print management

  • Timescales for getting an initial advertising leaflet printed were too small, resulting in leaflets not getting printed and posted out
    • Leaflet was sent out in PDF via email to all relevant organisations
    • The actual need for a leaflet needs discussing, event sold out regardless of leaflet not being completed. However, getting a leaflet out at the right time might result in earlier ticket purchases, instead of later/last minute purchases.
  • There was a distinct lack of people to take on doing actual design work, finding out about this issue late in the game, I then spent several evenings rushing to get both leaflet and booklet complete. If I had realised earlier on, I would have put time into managing that better. I plan to take on more of that role for 2014.
  • Booklet production was tight, I had to call in a favour with my local printers in order to get a turn around quick enough to get the booklets printed to bring with me to EMF
    • This seemed to extend from delays in getting the content correct, but also tied in with turn around times for getting booklets printed in time.
  • The website was fully functional, and worked a treat, I was asked to improve the site visually, but ran out of time.

General Notes

  • Due to issues with the badges, some of the workshops were cancelled and moved around. However over at the Nottinghack tent we were getting sent lots of people, who thought they had been sent over for the soldering workshop, I believe they were told that the workshop was cancelled, but there was soldering going on in the Nottinghack tent. This caused an issue for us as we did not have the scope, space or people on hand to be able to cope with that amount of people
    • Most people were more than pleasant with a lot of people coming back to do kits when we were less busy.
    • Ideally, the workshops should have been cancelled on the time tables posted online.
